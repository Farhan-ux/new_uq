# Research Report: Novel Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) Methods for LLM Factuality

## Executive Summary
This research explored 5 novel black-box UQ methods based on diverse mathematical theories: Fuzzy Sets, Dempster-Shafer Evidence Theory, Possibility Theory, Interval Probabilities (P-Boxes), and Bayesian GMM Evidence. The objective was to surpass previous SOTA benchmarks (AMC/SAME) in detecting LLM hallucinations without access to model internals.

## Evaluated Methods

### 1. Fuzzy Sets Theory (Fuzzy Entropy)
- **Concept:** Measures the "vagueness" of a response's membership in the semantic consensus set.
- **Performance:** AUROC ~0.50. Found to be highly sensitive to semantic dispersion, often conflating natural language variation with hallucination.

### 2. Dempster-Shafer Evidence Theory
- **Concept:** Assigns probability masses to "Belief", "Conflict", and "Ambiguity" based on pairwise semantic agreement.
- **Performance:** AUROC ~0.57. Stronger at identifying "Conflicting" hallucinations (contradictory responses) but struggled with "Consensus Hallucinations".

### 3. Possibility Theory (Necessity Measure)
- **Concept:** Uses the "Necessity" measure $N(A)$ to quantify the degree to which a response is necessarily factual given the distribution of possibilities.
- **Performance:** AUROC ~0.58. Showed good robustness across Llama models.

### 4. Interval Probabilities (P-Boxes)
- **Concept:** Represents uncertainty as an interval between upper and lower Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDFs).
- **Performance:** **AUROC ~0.61 (Best Individual Performance)**. Effectively captured the "aleatoric" uncertainty in model responses.

### 5. Bayesian Model Evidence (GMM-BIC)
- **Concept:** Uses the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) to determine if a response ensemble is better explained by a single factual cluster or multiple hallucinatory modes.
- **Performance:** AUROC ~0.55.
